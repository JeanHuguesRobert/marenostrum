# CONSTELLIA: A FRACTAL ARCHITECTURE FOR ENERGY, FOOD, AND DIGITAL SOVEREIGNTY IN ISLAND TERRITORIES

**Jean Hugues Noël Robert** and **Guillermo Valdes**  
Institut Mariani / C.O.R.S.I.C.A.

## ABSTRACT

Island territories suffer chronic structural dependency on imported energy, food, and digital infrastructure, creating cascading vulnerabilities in an increasingly fragmented global order. This paper presents *Constellia*, a fractal and modular architecture designed to transform islands into autonomous, interconnected sovereignty hubs. Each island operates as a semi-autonomous *galaxy* integrating agrivoltaic energy production, multi-form storage, and distributed AI computation within a unified governance and sovereign finance framework. Galaxies interconnect through *Stella*, a decentralised energy–data routing network inspired by ARPANET survivability principles. The financial backbone relies on island sovereign funds (*Galassia* model) enabling progressive self-financing through three revenue streams: energy and mobile storage export, AI computation services, and synthetic fuels. The architecture reframes food sovereignty not as autarky but as intelligent interdependence — islands specialise in high-value energy and compute production while managing strategic food reserves and supply-chain redundancy through structured bilateral exchange. The *Corsica2038* case study is grounded in verified PVGIS field data: 30,000 ha of vertical bifacial east-west agrivoltaic panels yield ~10.8 GWp of installed capacity, producing ~16.0 TWh/year net — 7.4 times local consumption — with a surplus of ~13.8 TWh/year convertible into sovereign AI compute at €350–2,800/MWh-equivalent, generating €3–12 billion/year in recoverable margin. The *Cuba2038* case illustrates high-potential scaling across a heterogeneous political and geographic context. Constellia inverts the periphery–centre paradigm: structurally dependent territories become strategically sovereign nodes in a distributed planetary resilience grid.

**Keywords:** island sovereignty; agrivoltaics; vertical bifacial panels; pumped hydro storage; distributed AI computing; mobile battery containers; synthetic fuels; fractal infrastructure; ARPANET resilience; sovereign fund; food sovereignty; intelligent interdependence.

## 1. INTRODUCTION

Island territories represent approximately 10% of the world's nations yet are disproportionately exposed to energy import dependency, food supply vulnerability, and digital infrastructure centralisation. These structural conditions create systemic risks that compound under climate stress, supply-chain disruption, or geopolitical fragmentation (IRENA, 2023). Recent events have demonstrated the acuity of this exposure: island and peninsula territories whose energy supply depends on a single cable or a single tanker have proven among the most rapidly destabilised under external pressure.

The conventional response — gradual renewable deployment within existing continental dependency frameworks — addresses symptoms rather than structure. This paper proposes a more fundamental reorientation. Islands possess intrinsic assets that continental territories cannot replicate: defined geographic boundaries enabling system-level optimisation; disproportionately high solar exposure relative to land area; physical isolation that, typically perceived as a liability, becomes a security asset for sovereign data infrastructure; and governance scale compatible with direct-distribution economic models that are impossible to implement at continental scale.

*Constellia* formalises these advantages into a unified fractal architecture combining energy production, multi-form storage, distributed AI computation, and sovereign finance. The framework introduces three conceptual contributions:

1. **Energy-to-compute conversion as primary value chain**: island surplus solar energy is not exported as low-value electricity but converted into high-value AI computation services, achieving a ×4–31 value multiplier on the same physical resource.

2. **Intelligent food interdependence**: food sovereignty is not autarky but specialised exchange — islands focus on high-value energy and compute production while managing strategic agricultural reserves and supply redundancy through contractual interdependence with complementary territories. Economic leverage, not self-sufficiency, is the operative mechanism of resilience.

3. **ARPANET-inspired resilience topology**: a decentralised network of independent nodes ensures that no single point of failure can cascade into system-wide collapse — a design principle directly applicable to both energy infrastructure and defence doctrine (Baran, 1964).

The paper is structured as follows. Section 2 presents the system architecture across its five layers. Section 3 develops quantitative modelling grounded in the *Corsica2038* real-data case study. Section 4 presents comparative case studies. Section 5 discusses geopolitical and policy implications. Section 6 concludes.

## 2. SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE

### 2.1 Fractal structure

Constellia adopts a five-layer recursive configuration in which each layer replicates the structural logic of those above and below it, enabling genuine scalability: a galaxy can be instantiated on any island with solar resources above ~1,400 kWh/kWp/year, with parameters adjusted to local morphology.

| Layer | Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Global | **Constellia** | Network of autonomous island systems |
| Territorial | **Galaxy** | Island-level sovereign platform (e.g., Corsica2038, Cuba2038) |
| Network | **Stella** | Distributed energy–data routing fabric |
| Financial | **Galassia / Galaxia** | Sovereign and cooperative investment vehicles |
| Operational | **Modular units** | Containerised batteries, data centres, PtL units |

The Stella layer enables cross-galaxy energy balancing: a galaxy in surplus exports charged battery containers to a galaxy under seasonal stress, receiving synthetic fuel in return. This is a physical instantiation of distributed packet routing — energy becomes logistically liquid.

### 2.2 Energy production and four-layer storage

Each galaxy integrates diversified generation and storage across four temporal layers designed to achieve full operational autonomy regardless of meteorological conditions:

**Layer 0 — Continuous production (agrivoltaic)**  
Vertical bifacial east-west panels deployed across agricultural land. The east-west orientation produces a flattened daily generation profile with dual peaks — morning and evening — that matches data centre 24/7 demand better than south-facing systems, reducing battery storage requirements by approximately 30%. A Ground Coverage Ratio (GCR) of 30–40% leaves 60–70% of soil accessible for compatible agriculture, including grazing, cereals, lavender, and arboriculture. This is not a land-use trade-off — it is a land-use optimisation whose agronomic benefits are empirically documented (Dupraz et al., 2011; Weselek et al., 2019).

**Layer 1 — Intraday buffer: 0 to 15 minutes (V2G fleet)**  
The electrified vehicle fleet operates in Vehicle-to-Grid mode, acting as a distributed, invisible battery. There is no storage silo to destroy and no single cable to cut. Rack-level supercapacitors provide sub-second response for data centre power quality.

**Layer 2 — Daily to weekly storage: 15 minutes to 16 hours (STEP + Li-ion)**  
Pumped hydro energy storage exploits island topography — Mediterranean island relief typically provides more than 1,000 m of vertical drop within 20 km — delivering multi-hour bulk storage at competitive cost (IRENA, 2023). Ground-mounted Li-ion batteries provide sub-hour ramping and arbitrage.

**Layer 3 — Strategic reserve: days to months (Power-to-Liquid)**  
E-diesel and SAF synthesised from summer solar surplus via the Power-to-Liquid pathway (Sternberg and Bardow, 2015). E-diesel stores stably in standard steel tanks without degradation or cycle limitation. A 90-day strategic reserve at 600 MW of load provides full energy autonomy through worst-case winter anticyclonic episodes.

**Mobile battery containers** constitute the physical Stella routing layer. Standard 40-foot containers housing 2–4 MWh LFP battery packs with bidirectional inverters connect to any grid node within two hours and travel by standard ro-ro ferry. In peacetime, they are exported to continental grids in exchange for e-diesel produced at continental PtL facilities. In crisis, they are recalled. This mechanism embodies the Stella principle: energy as a packet, territory as a router.

### 2.3 Distributed AI computation layer — the value multiplier

Surplus energy is not sold at commodity prices. It is converted into AI computation services at sovereign premium pricing. This value chain inversion is the defining economic insight of Constellia:

| Service tier | Equivalent price | Multiplier | Target market |
|---|---|---|---|
| Raw electricity export | €90/MWh | ×1 | EPEX spot |
| Batch AI compute (no SLA) | €350/MWh | ×4 | Model training |
| Premium EU — SLA 99.9% | €1,100/MWh | ×12 | Enterprise inference |
| Sovereign critical | €2,800/MWh | ×31 | Defence, health, finance |

Containerised GPU data centres (40–120 kW/rack, PUE 1.05–1.35 with seawater cooling at depth) are co-located with generation hubs. The Stella topology provides ARPANET-inspired redundant routing: each data centre node is independent; destroying 30% of nodes reduces compute capacity by 30% without cascade failure. Five-nines SLA (99.999% uptime) is achieved through the four-layer storage architecture described above.

The sovereign compute premium is not speculative. EU AI Act compliance, national security certification (SecNumCloud, ANSSI), and physical inaccessibility — island geography with no land border — combine into a value proposition that continental hyperscalers structurally cannot replicate. Physical defensibility is an intrinsic property of island infrastructure that no continental data centre can purchase.

### 2.4 Food sovereignty — intelligent interdependence

Constellia rejects the false binary between food autarky and unconditional import dependency. The operative principle is **intelligent interdependence**: islands specialise in their highest-value comparative advantage — energy and compute production — while maintaining three interlocking food security mechanisms.

First, **strategic reserves**: minimum 90-day critical food stocks managed at galaxy level, sufficient to cover any plausible supply disruption scenario.

Second, **agrivoltaic food production**: the 30,000 ha supporting solar panels simultaneously produces forage, cereals, aromatic plants, and fruit. INRAE field trials demonstrate that vertical bifacial panels improve summer crop microclimate through evapo-transpiration reduction, benefiting wheat, lavender, and grassland yields (Marrou et al., 2013). In the Corsican context, high-value agricultural specialties — Muscat, chestnut, olive — are demonstrably compatible with partial agrivoltaic shade.

Third, **contractual supply redundancy**: bilateral agreements with complementary territories guarantee food supply continuity in exchange for energy or compute credits. This is structured mutual assurance, not dependency.

Food sovereignty in the Constellia model means: *we choose what we import, when, from whom, and at what price — because our energy and compute exports give us the economic leverage to negotiate from strength.* An island generating €5–12 billion per year in sovereign revenues has a qualitatively different food security position than one dependent on subsidised imports, regardless of local production volumes.

### 2.5 Governance and finance — the Galassia sovereign fund model

Drawing on the Norwegian Government Pension Fund Global and the Danish Vattenfall cooperative model, the Galassia fund (Corsica) and Galaxia fund (Cuba) share four structural principles:

- **Ownership rule**: no external shareholder may hold more than 5% of any galaxy asset without parliamentary approval by the island legislature.
- **Direct dividend**: annual distribution to all island tax residents with more than three years of continuous residency. Target for Corsica2038: €500/month by 2038.
- **Reinvestment rule**: 30% of annual revenues reinvested into infrastructure, creating self-compounding capitalisation.
- **Anti-capture governance**: board members selected by civic lottery (Irish citizens' assembly model); full public audit annually; any asset disposal exceeding €10M subject to local referendum.

Financing follows a three-phase sequence: green bonds guaranteed by the national state (2026–2028, ~€500M); reinvestment of revenues combined with European Investment Bank green debt (2028–2032, €3–5B); full self-financing with zero external debt from 2032 onward.

## 3. QUANTITATIVE MODELLING — CORSICA2038 CASE STUDY

### 3.1 Agrivoltaic energy balance

All parameters are grounded in PVGIS satellite data for Corsica (JRC, 2023a; JRC, 2023b) and the French national agrivoltaic research programme (INRAE/Sun'Agri; Fraunhofer ISE, 2023).

| Parameter | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Available agricultural land | 30,000 ha | Field survey |
| Effective agrivoltaic fraction | 70% → 21,000 ha | Land-use planning |
| Panel height | 2.5 m | Technical specification |
| Inter-row pitch | 7.0 m | Agronomic optimisation |
| Ground Coverage Ratio | 35.7% | GCR = 2.5 / 7.0 |
| Panel power density | 220 Wp/m² | Market standard 2025 |
| Bifacial gain (albedo ~0.22) | +18% | Fraunhofer ISE (2023) |
| Capacity factor — PVGIS Corsica E-O vertical | 18.8% | JRC PVGIS (2023b) |
| System losses | 10% | Industry standard |
| **Installed capacity** | **10.8 GWp** | Calculated |
| **Gross annual production** | **17.8 TWh/year** | 10.8 × 0.188 × 8,760 |
| **Net annual production** | **16.0 TWh/year** | × (1 − 0.10) |
| Corsica local consumption | 2.2 TWh/year | OREGES (2023) |
| **Net surplus for export / compute** | **13.8 TWh/year** | Net − local consumption |

*Note on capacity factor*: 18.8% CF for vertical E-O bifacial panels is the PVGIS-validated figure for Mediterranean Corsica, corresponding to ~1,650 kWh/kWp/year. South-facing 30° systems achieve approximately 22% CF (~1,950 kWh/kWp/year) at the same location. The E-O configuration sacrifices ~15% of peak annual yield but delivers a significantly flatter daily production profile — dual morning/evening peaks rather than a single midday maximum — that reduces battery storage requirements for continuous-load data centre operation by approximately 30%. This operational advantage partly offsets the yield difference in the total system economics.

### 3.2 Strategic significance of 10.8 GWp

To contextualise the scale of the Corsica2038 deployment:

| Benchmark | Value |
|---|---|
| French national solar fleet, end of 2023 | ~22 GWp |
| Corsica2038 agrivoltaic fleet | 10.8 GWp on 0.6% of French territory |
| GPU equivalent (40 kW/rack, PUE 1.35) | ~230,000 racks / ~1.8 million H100-equivalent |
| Multiple of Corsica local consumption | 7.4× |
| Ratio to Corsica land area | 21,000 ha / 870,000 ha total = 2.4% |

This is not a marginal energy project. It is a structural reorientation of a territory's economic base, comparable in scale and ambition to Norway's decision to industrialise its continental shelf hydrocarbon resources in the 1970s.

## 4. COMPARATIVE CASE STUDIES

### 4.1 Corsica2038 — constrained pilot, EU governance benchmark

Corsica is characterised by constrained agrivoltaic density due to landscape integration policies, AOC zone requirements, and heritage landscape protection — all of which favour vertical bifacial configuration as the technically optimal and politically acceptable solution (JRC, 2023a). Institutional stability, EU legal framework, and proximity to European decision centres make it the natural regulatory pilot for the Constellia architecture.

Key accelerator: acquisition of the existing operator Corsica Sole (~200 MWp operational, active HTB grid connections, 10 years of verified production data, existing environmental permits) reduces deployment timeline by 5–7 years compared to greenfield development. The FSEC and the Collectivité de Corse acting as co-acquirers prevents external capture while providing institutional legitimacy.

**Role in Constellia**: demonstrating EU AI Act and SecNumCloud ANSSI compliance; establishing the Galassia governance template; providing the empirical production dataset for PVGIS validation at galaxy scale.

### 4.2 Cuba2038 — high-potential scaling, Caribbean hub

Cuba presents complementary characteristics that make it the natural second deployment: solar resource of 1,900–2,100 kWh/kWp/year; significant pumped hydro potential in the Sierra Maestra range; approximately 800,000 ha suitable for high-density agrivoltaic deployment; and a structural energy dependency of approximately 80% on fossil fuel imports whose disruption has repeatedly demonstrated systemic fragility.

The strategic position in Caribbean shipping lanes creates a natural market for SAF and e-diesel production targeting Atlantic aviation and maritime decarbonisation under ReFuelEU-equivalent mandates. The Latin American sovereign cloud market — currently dominated by US hyperscalers — represents a multi-billion-dollar opportunity for certified sovereign AI computation that no EU-based operator currently addresses.

**Role in Constellia**: proof of replicability beyond the EU regulatory context; SAF and e-diesel production hub for Atlantic corridors; AI compute export hub for Latin American sovereign digital markets.

### 4.3 Generalisation conditions

A Constellia galaxy is viable when three conditions are simultaneously satisfied:

1. Solar resource ≥ 1,400 kWh/kWp/year
2. Agricultural land availability ≥ 5,000 ha suitable for agrivoltaics
3. Governance stability sufficient for 20-year infrastructure contracts

These conditions define a deployable territory of approximately 40–60 island states and autonomous regions globally, representing a combined population of roughly 80 million people currently in structural energy dependency.

## 5. DISCUSSION AND IMPLICATIONS

### 5.1 The value inversion — from photon to sovereign token

The central economic insight of Constellia is a value chain inversion invisible in conventional energy planning. A photon hitting a bifacial panel on a Corsican hillside generates identical physical energy in three scenarios — but ×1, ×12, or ×31 economic value depending on whether it is sold as raw electricity, premium EU compute, or sovereign-certified AI computation respectively.

This inversion has a historical precedent that clarifies the strategic logic. Switzerland never extracted an ounce of gold. It built the vaults, the governance, and the trust infrastructure that made everyone else's gold worth more by being there. The AI token is the gold of the twenty-first century. Island territories with the right architecture — sovereign, resilient, physically certified — can become its custodians.

### 5.2 The cognitive arms race dimension

Beyond the commercial compute market, the convergence of AI capability and energy infrastructure carries a defence dimension that conventional energy policy frameworks do not address. In a context of accelerating cognitive arms competition, sovereign computation capacity — physically inviolable, energetically autonomous, geographically defended — constitutes a strategic asset of a qualitatively different nature from commodity cloud computing. The physical defensibility of island infrastructure is not a metaphor: it is an architectural property that continental data centres cannot replicate through software or certification alone.

The ARPANET analogy, again, is instructive. Paul Baran's 1964 design principle was military before it was commercial. The Stella network inherits this genealogy deliberately.

### 5.3 Geopolitical implications

The Constellia architecture produces a structural shift in the hierarchy of strategic assets. Island territories that implement the full galaxy architecture transition from passive nodes — dependent consumers at the periphery of continental systems — to active sovereign hubs capable of providing critical services that continental powers require.

This transition creates negotiating leverage that island territories have historically lacked. An island that produces surplus sovereign-certified AI computation is not asking continental powers for subsidies — it is offering them something they need and cannot produce elsewhere on equivalent terms. The relationship inverts.

At the network level, a constellation of Constellia galaxies — Corsica, Cuba, Réunion, Martinique, Pacific island states — constitutes a distributed sovereign infrastructure grid that operates independently of any single continental power's control. This is not an adversarial architecture; it is a resilient one. Its value to any continental partner is precisely its independence.

### 5.4 Limitations and future research

This paper presents a systems-level architecture grounded in verified energy data but requiring further quantitative development in several dimensions:

- **Multi-agent energy flow simulation**: dynamic modelling of the four storage layers under real Corsican meteorological sequences (TMY data from PVGIS) to validate SLA commitment under worst-case conditions.
- **Agrivoltaic food system modelling**: quantitative crop yield analysis under vertical bifacial conditions for Corsican and Cuban agro-climatic zones, extending the INRAE dataset to Mediterranean summer stress conditions.
- **Sovereign fund capitalisation dynamics**: formal modelling of FSEC/Galaxia accumulation under different revenue distribution and reinvestment rules over 20-year horizons.
- **Network topology resilience**: formal graph-theoretic analysis of Stella routing performance under adversarial node removal scenarios, extending the qualitative ARPANET analogy into quantitative network science.

## 6. CONCLUSION

Constellia provides a framework for transforming island territories into integrated systems combining renewable energy, storage, and distributed AI computation. Through its fractal and modular architecture, the system enables scalability, operational resilience, and progressive autonomy.

By directly coupling energy generation with computational capacity, Constellia shifts infrastructure from a consumption-based model to a production-oriented system spanning both physical and informational domains. This integration allows territories to establish locally controlled value chains and reduce structural dependency on external systems.

The dual deployment pathway — Corsica as a constrained regulatory pilot within the EU framework, and Cuba as a high-potential scaling environment — demonstrates the adaptability and replicability of the architecture across heterogeneous political and geographic contexts.

Food sovereignty, in this model, is not autarky but intelligent interdependence: territories specialise in their highest-value comparative advantage — sovereign energy and computation — while managing strategic reserves and structured bilateral exchange. Economic leverage, not self-sufficiency, is the operative mechanism of resilience.

More broadly, Constellia contributes to the emergence of distributed infrastructures characterised by autonomy, redundancy, and interoperability — an architecture conceived, like ARPANET before it, to survive the failure of any single node.

In this perspective, island territories cease to be peripheral nodes in systems they do not control. The token becomes the new gold. The island becomes the vault.
