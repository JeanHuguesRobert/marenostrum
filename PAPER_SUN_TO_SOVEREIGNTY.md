## Abstract

Tourism-dependent territories face a structural contradiction: the same flows that generate economic value also drive land dispossession through differential purchasing power. In high-attractivity, land-constrained regions — particularly islands — external demand progressively converts transient flows (visitors) into durable control over land (ownership). Conventional instruments such as tourist taxes capture only a marginal share of value and do not address this flow-to-stock conversion.

This paper proposes a systemic alternative: **Communal Sovereign Funds (CSF)** that transform locally abundant renewable energy — primarily solar — into financial flows, and subsequently into non-speculative land and housing assets. By structurally reinvesting energy-derived revenues (including high-grade AI inference services produced from solar photons) into land acquisition and decommodified housing, CSFs aim to restore local purchasing power symmetry and prevent territorial extraction. The paper grounds this proposal in the Corsican case, where detailed fiscal data (Agence du Tourisme de la Corse, 2026) reveals a structural gap between tourist tax collection (14.2 M€ in 2023) and its theoretical potential (~33 M€), while 36.4 million tourist nights annually and 351 M€ in short-term rental revenues demonstrate both the scale of tourism flows and the magnitude of their fiscal under-capture.

Positioned at the intersection of ecological economics, tourism studies, and distributed infrastructure, this work reframes overtourism as a capital allocation problem rather than a capacity problem, and introduces an energy-to-land conversion mechanism — the photon-to-inference chain — as a pathway to territorial sovereignty.

## 1. Introduction

Tourism is commonly framed as an economic opportunity for peripheral regions. However, in high-attractivity territories with constrained land supply, it produces a secondary structural effect: the progressive transfer of property rights from local populations to external actors. This phenomenon is particularly visible in island contexts, where land is finite and non-expandable, demand is global while income is local, economic diversification is structurally limited, and capital mobility exceeds regulatory capacity.

Corsica exemplifies this dynamic with unusual clarity. The island recorded 36.4 million tourist nights in 2023 (ATC, 2026), ranking it among the most intensely visited Mediterranean territories relative to its permanent population (~340,000). Over 52% of total tourist accommodation capacity — some 211,911 beds across 32,225 active listings — consists of unclassified private rentals commercialised via digital platforms such as Airbnb and Abritel (ATC/AirDNA, 2026). These platforms generated an estimated 351 million euros in rental revenues in 2023 alone. Yet the total tourist tax (taxe de séjour) collected by all nineteen Corsican intercommunal bodies reached only 14.16 M€ — representing roughly 43% of a theoretically attainable 33 M€ and less than 4.1% of platform rental revenues (ATC, 2026).

This disproportion is not accidental. It reflects a structural feature of existing fiscal instruments: they are designed to capture flows, but territorial transformation operates at the stock level. External actors convert tourist spending into land ownership; local authorities collect a levy on the visit but not on the transfer of territorial control that follows.

This paper argues that the core issue is not tourism itself, but the **conversion of transient economic flows into durable control over land**. It then proposes a class of institutions — Communal Sovereign Funds — that redirect a locally abundant resource (solar energy) through a value chain ending in collective, non-speculative land ownership. The Corsican context serves both as the primary empirical case and as the proposed site of institutional innovation.

## 2. The Structural Limits of Conventional Instruments

### 2.1 Tourist Taxation: An Instrument Designed for the Wrong Variable

Tourist taxes (taxe de séjour in France, analogous levies in Italy, Spain, Greece) operate on a simple logic: visitors who impose costs on host territories should contribute financially to offsetting them. Instituted in France by the law of 13 January 1910, originally to help thermal resorts compete with German and Austro-Hungarian alternatives, the taxe de séjour has retained its philosophical architecture across a century of legislative evolution: it is a per-night, per-person levy on non-resident accommodation, affectée by law to tourism-related expenditure (ATC, 2026, §1.2).

The instrument has genuine merits. It is transparent, administratively delegatable (since 2019, digital platforms collect it as mandatory intermediaries), and its Corsican yield has grown substantially: from 8.78 M€ in 2021 to 14.16 M€ in 2023, a 61% increase in two years (ATC, 2026, §4.1). In Corsica, all nineteen EPCI have adopted the maximum applicable rate of 5% for unclassified rentals, augmented by the 10% taxe additionnelle départementale directed to the ATC (ATC, 2026, §1.6; §4.2.6).

Yet this progress reveals rather than resolves the structural problem. The ATC's own analysis estimates a theoretical collection potential of approximately 33 M€ per year at current rates and volumes — meaning the current gap represents some 19 M€ in annual uncaptured fiscal capacity, even before questioning whether the rates themselves are adequate (ATC, 2026, §4.2.7). More fundamentally, even full collection at current rates would represent less than 10% of the 351 M€ in platform rental revenues identified by AirDNA for 2023 (ATC, 2026, §4.2.6). The instrument is calibrated to finance tourist infrastructure, not to balance the capital dynamics that tourism sets in motion.

Three structural limitations explain why tourist taxation cannot, by design, address land dispossession.

**First, the magnitude is insufficient.** Even at theoretical maximum, 33 M€ per year against an annual rental revenue stream of 351 M€ — itself a fraction of total real estate transactions driven by tourism attractivity — cannot constitute a countervailing force in land markets. Corsican real estate prices have, over the past two decades, tracked Mediterranean demand rather than local wages.

**Second, the instrument targets flows while the strategic variable is stocks.** Tax revenues, even when formally earmarked, are spent on annual tourism expenditures. They do not accumulate into permanent territorial assets. A visitor who pays the tourist tax this summer may return next summer as a property owner — and at that point, the fiscal instrument ceases to apply while the territorial effect persists indefinitely.

**Third, there is a discontinuity at the visitor-to-owner transition.** The taxe de séjour reaches only those who do not own local property. Once the conversion from visitor to landowner occurs — the precise mechanism driving territorial transformation — the instrument becomes blind to it.

### 2.2 The Flow–Stock Asymmetry: A Formal Statement

Let $F$ denote the annual financial flows generated by tourism (accommodation revenues, hospitality spending, transport) and $S$ the stock of land and housing controlled by local versus non-local actors. Public policy instruments overwhelmingly target $F$ — through taxation, regulation of short-term rentals, accommodation classification requirements. The strategic variable, however, is $S$.

If external actors systematically convert a portion of $F$ into increments of $S$ through property acquisition, and local actors lack equivalent capital to do the same, then:

$$\Delta S_{external} \gg \Delta S_{local}$$

over any sustained period, irrespective of the level of taxation on $F$. The structure of the problem is one of differential capital formation capacity, not of insufficient regulatory intervention on tourism flows.

Conventional tourist taxation addresses $F$ without building local $S$. What is needed is an instrument that builds local $S$ directly — and does so from a resource base that is both locally abundant and impervious to external appropriation.

### 2.3 Digital Platforms and the Governance Gap

The rise of short-term rental platforms (Airbnb, Abritel/Vrbo) has deepened the flow–stock asymmetry in a specific way. By lowering transaction costs for short-term rentals, they have dramatically expanded the stock of housing effectively mobilised for tourism, converting what were previously primary or secondary residences into permanent tourism infrastructure. In Corsica, the 32,225 active platform listings represent 52% of total tourist accommodation capacity — a structural dominance of the informal-private segment that no other Mediterranean island of comparable scale has yet adequately regulated (ATC, 2026, §3.2).

The legislative response — mandatory fiscal intermediation for platforms (loi Élan, 2019; FARITAS project, 2024) — has improved tax collection without addressing the underlying conversion mechanism. Airbnb's reversements to Corsican communes reached 3.9 M€ in 2023 (ATC, 2026, §2.2), a non-trivial but structurally marginal sum relative to the capital dynamics at play. Portivechju and Aiacciu appear in the national top-50 of communes by Airbnb tax receipts — an indicator of tourism intensity, not of adequate fiscal capture.

## 3. Comparative Context: The Island Condition

### 3.1 Structural Features of Island Tourism Economies

Island economies share a configuration that systematically amplifies the flow–stock asymmetry identified above. Finite land supply sets a hard constraint on the response of local housing stock to demand increases: unlike continental metropolitan areas where urban expansion can partly absorb external demand, islands cannot expand their territory. Exposure to global tourism flows means that demand is priced in international purchasing power while local incomes are anchored to regional labour markets. Economic dependence on tourism revenue simultaneously creates pressure to accommodate visitor demand and weakens the political capacity to impose restrictive counter-measures.

Tourism thus acts simultaneously as an economic engine and as a vector of vulnerability — a dual character that has been extensively documented across Mediterranean, Atlantic, and Pacific island contexts (Figueroa & Rotarou, 2021; Blazevic, 2023).

### 3.2 Comparative Cases

Similar dynamics are observable across multiple island territories. The Canary Islands have documented severe housing pressure in coastal municipalities driven by speculative short-term rental conversion and non-resident acquisition (Almeida-García, 2025). The Balearic Islands present among the highest tourist-to-resident ratios in Europe, with Mallorca recording a non-resident ownership share exceeding 20% in coastal municipalities. Hawaii has experienced a decades-long process of land price inflation driven by mainland and international acquisition that has displaced native Hawaiian populations from coastal zones. Iceland, a more recent case, illustrates the speed with which tourism intensification can generate infrastructure strain and fiscal capacity gaps, even in a territory with relatively strong public institutions (Tverijonaite, 2024).

These cases converge on a common mechanism: an inflow of external purchasing power raises accommodation returns, incentivises conversion of residential housing to short-term rental use, prices the local housing stock at external demand levels, and progressively excludes local residents from both ownership and affordable rental markets.

### 3.3 Redefining the Problem: Overtourism as Capital Misallocation

Standard definitions of overtourism focus on visitor intensity — the ratio of visitors to residents, infrastructure load, environmental impact. This paper proposes a complementary interpretation: overtourism is a symptom of a deeper structural imbalance, specifically the uncontrolled conversion of transient flows into permanent ownership. By this definition, the relevant metric is not the number of visitors per square kilometre but the rate at which external capital is converting local housing stock into non-local assets. A territory could, in principle, sustain high visitor numbers without territorial dispossession if adequate institutions were in place to intercept the flow-to-stock conversion mechanism. The absence of such institutions — not tourism volume per se — is the pathological variable.

## 4. Theoretical Framework

### 4.1 Purchasing Power Differential and Land Price Formation

Land prices in open economies converge toward the highest sustainable demand. In territories exposed to both local and external purchasing power, this implies:

$$P_{land} \sim \max(PP_{external}, PP_{local})$$

When $PP_{external} \gg PP_{local}$ — as is structurally the case in Corsica, where average annual incomes lag behind the French national median while buyers from Northern Europe, Paris, and the French Riviera bring metropolitan purchasing power — external ownership becomes structurally inevitable under purely market conditions. No volume of tourist taxation at current rates can bridge a purchasing power differential of this magnitude. The solution must operate at the capital formation level, not at the tax capture level.

### 4.2 Energy as Endogenous Economic Substrate

All economic value is ultimately rooted in energy transformation. This physical grounding, long recognised in ecological economics (Georgescu-Roegen, 1971; Daly, 1996), acquires operational significance when a territory possesses an abundant, locally controlled, and renewable energy resource that can be transformed into market-valued outputs.

Solar irradiation in the Mediterranean constitutes such a resource. Corsica receives between 2,600 and 2,900 hours of sunshine annually — among the highest values in Western Europe — across an island where some 30,000 hectares of agricultural land (friches viticoles, former vineyards abandoned following the post-1975 agricultural restructuring) lie economically underutilised and are structurally unsuited to conventional agricultural recovery. The conjunction of abundant solar resource and available land constitutes a latent economic substrate of considerable magnitude.

We define the following transformation chain:

$$E \rightarrow C \rightarrow R \rightarrow A$$

Where:
- $E$ = solar energy (photons captured by photovoltaic panels)
- $C$ = computation — specifically, AI inference services produced by sovereign data centres powered by this energy
- $R$ = revenues generated by the sale of inference capacity on European and global AI markets
- $A$ = territorial assets (land, housing) acquired with these revenues under non-speculative governance regimes

This chain — which we designate the **photon-to-inference pathway** — represents a structural innovation relative to conventional energy monetisation. Rather than exporting electricity at wholesale grid prices (the conventional approach, limited by interconnection capacity and subject to market price volatility), sovereign AI compute transforms solar energy into a high-value-added digital service. The value multiplier is substantial: electricity exported at wholesale Mediterranean prices (€40–80/MWh) versus AI inference compute sold at data centre rates (€0.20–2.00 per compute-hour equivalent) represents a ×10 to ×40 value amplification per unit of primary energy. This multiplier is the economic core of the CSF model proposed below.

## 5. Communal Sovereign Funds: Architecture and Mechanism

### 5.1 Definition and Governance Principles

A Communal Sovereign Fund (CSF) is a locally governed institutional entity with three defining characteristics. First, it aggregates revenues from the productive transformation of locally abundant resources — primarily but not exclusively solar energy — through the photon-to-inference pathway. Second, it reinvests these revenues systematically into territorial assets, principally land and housing, held under permanent communal or cooperative ownership. Third, it removes these assets from speculative markets through structural constraints on resale, use, and capitalisation, making them permanently affordable and locally controlled.

The CSF differs from a conventional public investment fund in its revenue base (endogenous productive capacity rather than tax extraction), its asset governance (structural decommodification rather than portfolio optimisation), and its territorial scope (place-based rather than sector-based). It differs from a standard cooperative or SCIC in its explicit accumulation objective: the progressive build-up of a territorial land base sufficient to counteract the purchasing power differential that drives external acquisition.

In the French legal framework, the CSF architecture is most naturally implemented through a tripartite structure: a SCIC SA (Société Coopérative d'Intérêt Collectif) as the operational entity, incorporating impartible reserves constituting the sovereign fund proper; a Fonds de Dotation as the vehicle for communal dividend distribution and public mission governance; and an OFS/BRS (Organisme de Foncier Solidaire / Bail Réel Solidaire) as the perpetual affordable housing mechanism. This structure aligns with recent French legislative evolution, including the loi Huwart (November 2025) which creates new derogation mechanisms for cooperative land development in territories experiencing acute housing pressure.

### 5.2 The Photon-to-Inference Value Chain in Detail

The operational architecture of the photon-to-inference pathway deserves detailed specification, as it constitutes the distinctive productive foundation of the CSF model.

**Solar capture.** Vertically oriented bifacial east-west photovoltaic panels deployed over former vineyard land offer a specific advantage in Mediterranean contexts: their vertical geometry reduces ground-level shading impact (compatible with agrivoltaic co-use), their east-west orientation produces a flattened generation profile that better matches data centre load curves than south-facing panels, and their bifacial design captures reflected irradiation from light-coloured soils. At Corsican irradiation levels, a deployment of 30,000 hectares (the estimated available friche viticole surface) with appropriate panel density would yield approximately 10.8 GWp of installed capacity, generating on the order of 16 TWh per year net of system losses.

**Sovereign compute.** Rather than exporting this electricity — an option constrained by the Corsican interconnection infrastructure (the SARCO submarine cable operates near capacity) and limited in value by wholesale electricity prices — the CSF routes it into sovereign AI data centres optimised for inference workloads. AI inference (the execution of trained models against user queries, as opposed to the training process) is energetically intensive, highly parallelisable, and produces a service that can be sold globally without physical transport. In the present framing, the relevant metric is the Compute eXergy Unit (CXU = E × η\_hw × η\_sys × η\_sla), where hardware, system, and service-level efficiencies modulate the economic value extractable from a given primary energy input. At realistic efficiency parameters, a 1 GWh input to sovereign AI compute yields revenues of the order of €0.5M–€2M depending on service tier — against €40,000–80,000 for the same gigawatt-hour exported to the continental grid.

**Revenue pooling and land conversion.** The CSF pools inference revenues and directs them to a land acquisition vehicle operating under OFS/BRS constraints. Land acquired in this way is permanently removed from speculative circulation: it can be used (residential usufruct under long-term lease) but not sold at market prices. Capital gains on improvements accrue to the fund, not to individual leaseholders. Over time, as the CSF's land base grows, its purchasing power in local real estate markets increases — potentially reaching the threshold at which it becomes a price-setting actor rather than a price-taking one.

### 5.3 Revenue Projections and Land Accumulation Dynamics

To illustrate the order of magnitude of the proposed mechanism, consider a conservative scenario calibrated to Corsican parameters. A first-phase deployment of 500 MW of agrivoltaic capacity (approximately 1,650 hectares of former vineyard land) at Corsican irradiation levels generates approximately 0.8 TWh/year of electrical output. Routing 60% of this output to sovereign AI compute at a conservative revenue rate of €0.7M/GWh yields annual inference revenues of approximately €336M. Even after operating costs, debt service on infrastructure financing, and cooperative dividend distributions, a CSF operating at this scale could accumulate €50M–€100M per year in land acquisition capacity — sufficient to acquire, at Corsican land prices, several hundred hectares of agricultural and peri-urban land annually.

At this accumulation rate, the CSF would reach the 5% land control threshold — estimated as the minimum for market stabilisation effects — within five to eight years of full operational deployment, and the 15% threshold associated with systemic price impact within fifteen to twenty years. These projections are indicative rather than predictive; they serve to demonstrate that the mechanism is within a realistic order of magnitude, not merely theoretically conceivable.

## 6. Mechanisms of Decommodification

### 6.1 Structural Separation of Ownership and Use Rights

The central innovation of the OFS/BRS model, on which the CSF housing mechanism rests, is the permanent separation of land ownership from residential use rights. The CSF owns the land; residents hold a Bail Réel Solidaire — a real right of use, heritable and transferable, but bounded by resale price constraints. This separation prevents the capitalisation of public subsidy into private wealth: when a BRS leaseholder sells their use right, they recover their improvements and a controlled fraction of real estate appreciation, while the land value increment accrues to the fund. The housing unit thus remains permanently affordable for successive occupants, rather than re-entering the speculative market after one generation.

This mechanism directly addresses the discontinuity problem identified in Section 2: unlike the tourist tax, which ceases to apply once a visitor becomes an owner, the BRS constraint persists indefinitely through ownership changes. Land acquired by the CSF is permanently removed from the speculative stock $S$, incrementing $S_{local}$ in a durable and irreversible way.

### 6.2 Primary Residence Requirements and Anti-Speculative Constraints

CSF-governed housing is restricted by governance covenant to primary residence use. This constraint, enforced through the OFS statutes, prevents the conversion of CSF-financed housing to short-term rental or seasonal use — the very vector of dispossession the fund is designed to counteract. Complementary constraints include limits on combined annual short-term rental days (aligned with or below the national 120-day threshold), preemption rights exercised by the CSF on any resale of use rights, and controlled capital gains formulae that track construction cost indices rather than speculative market values.

### 6.3 Governance and Democratic Accountability

A CSF is a territorial institution, not a financial vehicle. Its governance must therefore reflect the democratic character of the territory it serves. In the SCIC framework, this implies multi-stakeholder governance including elected communal and intercommunal representatives, resident leaseholder associations, workers in CSF-operated energy and compute activities, and civil society organisations with territorial missions. The SCIC's impartible reserves — the accumulated land and capital base — are constitutionally protected from distribution: they cannot be privatised by any majority decision, providing a structural safeguard against governance capture.

## 7. Integration with Distributed Infrastructure

The CSF model is architecturally congruent with the broader movement toward distributed, resilient infrastructure. Decentralised energy generation — rooftop photovoltaics, agrivoltaic arrays, small-scale wind — produces electricity at geographically distributed points rather than at centralised facilities. Edge computing extends the same logic to data processing: sovereign AI inference nodes deployed at energy generation sites eliminate the transmission losses and latency costs associated with centralised data centres, while capturing value locally rather than routing it to metropolitan operators. Mesh networks connecting these distributed nodes to local public services (health, education, administration) complete the infrastructure stack, enabling the CSF to operate not merely as a financial entity but as a territorial digital infrastructure provider.

This distributed architecture has specific advantages for island territories. It avoids dependence on finite interconnection capacity (Corsica's SARCO cable), reduces vulnerability to single points of failure, and enables progressive deployment aligned with investment capacity rather than requiring massive upfront centralised infrastructure. It also aligns with the European AI infrastructure policy agenda, which increasingly emphasises distributed sovereign compute as a strategic objective (AI Factories initiative, European AI Act, 2024).

## 8. Threshold Effects and Scaling Dynamics

The effectiveness of the CSF mechanism is non-linear in the share of land it controls. Let $\alpha$ denote the fraction of total territorial land (weighted by housing utility) held by the CSF under non-speculative governance.

For $\alpha < 0.05$ (below 5%), the CSF functions as a marginal actor in land markets. Its impact on local purchasing power symmetry is minimal, though it provides direct benefit to its leaseholders. This threshold can be reached in a first operational phase of three to five years.

For $0.05 < \alpha < 0.15$ (between 5% and 15%), the CSF begins to exert stabilising pressure on local land prices through the simple mechanism of market thinning: by removing a portion of land from speculative circulation, it reduces the supply available to external buyers and limits the upward pull of external purchasing power on the residual market. Simultaneously, the CSF's demonstrated financial capacity begins to affect the expectations of other actors, creating anticipatory effects.

For $\alpha > 0.15$ (above 15%), the CSF potentially becomes a price-setting actor rather than a price-taking one. At this scale, its revenues from inference services, combined with its accumulated land base as collateral, enable leverage at a magnitude sufficient to compete directly with external capital in real estate acquisition. The purchasing power differential that drives dispossession is structurally neutralised rather than merely attenuated.

These thresholds are empirical estimates derived from analogous cases in community land trust literature (Davis, 2010); their precise values in the Corsican context require calibration to local land market data.

## 9. Risk Analysis

### 9.1 Attractivity Feedback

A paradox latent in the CSF model deserves acknowledgement: by improving local living conditions, stabilising housing costs, and investing in territorial infrastructure, the fund may increase Corsica's attractivity as a place to live and visit — thereby reinforcing the external demand that drives dispossession. This feedback loop is real but manageable. The key structural feature is that CSF land, once acquired, is permanently non-speculative: increased attractivity raises the market value of the non-CSF land stock but leaves CSF-governed land outside the market. The solution lies in maintaining a sufficient accumulation rate relative to attractivity growth, and in applying complementary regulatory instruments (short-term rental caps, primary residence registration requirements) to slow the conversion of market-priced housing to tourism uses.

### 9.2 Revenue Volatility and Technology Risk

AI compute revenues are subject to market price evolution that is difficult to predict over decadal horizons. The emergence of more efficient inference architectures could reduce revenue per compute unit; conversely, explosive growth in AI service demand could increase it. The CSF model should therefore incorporate conservative revenue assumptions, diversify its productive base across energy sale, compute services, and agricultural output, and structure its land acquisition programme to be robust to downside scenarios. The physical land asset base, once acquired, is not subject to technology obsolescence.

### 9.3 Political and Governance Risk

A CSF operating at territorial scale is inevitably a political institution, subject to the risks of capture, mismanagement, and policy reversal. Structural safeguards include the SCIC's impartible reserve constraint, multi-stakeholder governance requirements, mandatory public auditing, and the OFS framework's constitutional protections. External accountability mechanisms — independent oversight boards, civil society participation, transparent reporting — are essential complements to formal governance structures.

### 9.4 Collection and Transition Risks

The proposed mechanism requires substantial upfront investment before generating revenues sufficient to fund land acquisition. Bridge financing — through green bonds, European cohesion funds, or regional public investment — is required during the deployment phase. The loi Huwart (November 2025) bail à construction mechanism provides a relevant instrument for initiating land access before full CSF capitalisation, by enabling long-term constructive leases that convert to ownership over time.

## 10. Discussion: From Regulation to Production

The CSF model represents a strategic reorientation of territorial policy in the face of tourism-driven dispossession. Conventional approaches — tourist taxation, short-term rental regulation, preemption rights — operate within the existing capital dynamics, attempting to restrain or redirect flows that are generated by mechanisms the territory does not control. The CSF approach reverses this logic: it establishes an endogenous productive capacity, generates revenues from a resource (solar energy) that is locally controlled and inexhaustible, and deploys those revenues to build the territorial asset base that conventional instruments cannot accumulate.

The core reframings are three. First, from taxation to production: the CSF generates income rather than levying it, aligning its financial interests with productive efficiency rather than with the volume of tourism it regulates. Second, from regulation to capital formation: rather than imposing constraints on external actors, the CSF builds local countervailing power through asset accumulation, operating in markets rather than against them. Third, from restriction to structural balance: the goal is not to limit tourism or external attractivity, but to ensure that a portion of the value these generate accrues permanently to the local territorial commons.

The mechanism is designed to enable coexistence of high attractivity — Corsica's Mediterranean identity, landscape, and cultural heritage are genuine goods that benefit visitors and residents alike — with territorial continuity, understood as the capacity of local communities to remain economically rooted in their own land across generations.

## 11. The Corsican Case: MareNostrum as a CSF Prototype

The framework presented above is being operationalised in Corsica under the designation **MareNostrum** (github.com/JeanHuguesRobert/marenostrum, CC BY-SA 4.0), a proposal developed by Institut Mariani in collaboration with Mediterranean research partners. MareNostrum treats Mediterranean solar irradiation as a democratic commons — physically available to Corsica, Sardinia, Sicily, Tunisia, and their neighbouring territories — and proposes a transnational architecture for converting this commons into sovereign AI compute capacity and, downstream, into territorial assets governed under principles analogous to those described above.

The historical analogy that grounds MareNostrum is the Charte du Soleil Méditerranéen, which draws on the 1951 European Coal and Steel Community as a precedent: just as the ECSC transformed energy resources that had fuelled continental conflict into a shared productive commons governed by supranational institutions, MareNostrum proposes that Mediterranean solar energy — currently under-utilised relative to its productive potential — be governed as a shared infrastructure producing sovereign AI compute as its primary value-added output. The narrative crystallised in the MareNostrum framework is precise: Switzerland kept its gold reserves as the foundation of monetary sovereignty; Corsica will keep its sunlight as the foundation of digital sovereignty. AI compute tokens become the new gold; the island's solar resource, rather than being exported as raw electrons at wholesale prices, is retained and transformed into high-value services whose revenues build local territorial power.

The MareNostrum framework's Compute eXergy Unit (CXU = E × η\_hw × η\_sys × η\_sla) provides the technical foundation for pricing sovereign compute services in a way that captures the full productive value of solar energy, rather than defaulting to grid electricity pricing that systematically undervalues Mediterranean irradiation relative to its productive potential. Institut Mariani is currently developing the governance, contractual, and pricing documentation for this framework (ARCHITECTURE.md, CONTRACTS.md, GOVERNANCE.md, MODEL.md, PRICING.md) and welcomes engagement from Mediterranean research institutions, energy cooperatives, and European digital sovereignty initiatives.

## 12. Conclusion

Tourism-driven territorial dispossession results from asymmetric capital dynamics, not from excessive visitor numbers. The flow-to-stock conversion mechanism — whereby external purchasing power, amplified by tourism attractivity, progressively acquires durable control over local land — operates independently of the volume of tourist tax collected and cannot be addressed by instruments calibrated to manage visitor flows rather than capital stocks.

Corsica's empirical situation illustrates this with particular clarity. A gap of ~19 M€ per year between actual and theoretical tourist tax collection is structurally irrelevant relative to 351 M€ in annual platform rental revenues and the capital dynamics of Mediterranean real estate markets. Closing this fiscal gap entirely would not alter the trajectory of external land acquisition.

Communal Sovereign Funds, capitalised through the photon-to-inference pathway, offer a mechanism of a different kind. They transform an infinite, locally abundant resource — solar energy — into revenues that build a finite, locally controlled resource: land held under permanent non-speculative governance. The core logic is convertibility: photons, captured by sovereign infrastructure, are converted into AI inference services, sold on global markets, and their revenues systematically converted into territorial assets that remain local in perpetuity.

The narrative proposition of this paper is therefore precise:

> *Convert an infinite resource (solar energy, via AI inference) into control over a finite one (land), and ensure that this control remains local, democratic, and permanent.*

This is not an argument against tourism; it is an argument for designing the institutions that allow tourism and territorial continuity to coexist. MareNostrum, as developed by Institut Mariani in Corte, represents a concrete proposal toward this institutional design.

### A.5 Theoretical Tourist Tax Recovery Potential — Corsica 2023

**Source: ATC 2026, §4.2 (Figures 15–18), calculs ATC sur bases INSEE, AirDNA, FVO**

#### A.5.1 Estimated tax per accommodation type

| Type d'hébergement | Nuitées prises en compte | Nuitées non prises en compte | TS théorique 2023 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hôtellerie classique | 2,3 M | 0,2 M | 3,37 M€ |
| HPA | 3,6 M | 0,3 M | 1,94 M€ |
| AHCT | 2,0 M | 0,9 M | 3,10 M€ |
| *Sous-total collectifs* | *7,9 M* | *1,4 M* | *8,41 M€* |
| Locatif classé/labellisé | ND | ND | ND |
| Locatif non classé (plateformes) | 6,7 M | 0 | **19,3 M€** |
| **Total pris en compte** | **14,6 M** | **1,4 M** | **27,71 M€** |

*Base de calcul pour le locatif non classé : 351 M€ de revenus × 5,5 % (taux maximal 5 % + TAD 10 %) = 19,3 M€. Tous les 19 EPCI corses ont adopté le taux plafond de 5 %.*

#### A.5.2 Recovery potential scenarios

| Scénario | Méthode | Estimation |
|---|---|---|
| **Plancher (A)** | Nuitées effectivement prises en compte (61 % du total marchand) | **27,71 M€** |
| **Intermédiaire** | Moyenne plancher/plafond | **~33 M€** |
| **Plafond (B)** | Extrapolation proportionnelle aux 39 % de nuitées non prises en compte | **38,2 M€** |

**Gap de recouvrement en 2023 : ~19 M€** (écart entre collecte effective de 14,16 M€ et estimation intermédiaire de 33 M€).

**Montant moyen de taxe de séjour par nuitée marchande** (base 23,7 M nuitées) : ~1,27 €/nuitée — qualifié par l'ATC d'« estimation réaliste, voire prudente ».
