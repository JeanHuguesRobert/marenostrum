# Mare Nostrum: Energy Sovereignty as Democratic Commons
## A Framework for Solar-Dominant Mediterranean Island Systems

**Jean Hugues Noël Robert, baron Mariani**
Institut Mariani R&D — C.O.R.S.I.C.A.
1 cours Paoli, F-20250 Corte, France
jhr@baronsmariani.org

*Policy Paper — Draft v0.1 — March 2026*
*Open draft: github.com/JeanHuguesRobert/marenostrum*

## Executive Summary

Mediterranean island territories — Corsica, Sardinia, Sicily, Tunisia — share three structural conditions: exceptional solar endowment, chronic energy dependence on external systems, and populations that receive none of the economic rent from the natural resources above their heads.

This paper proposes **Mare Nostrum**, a governance and economic framework that transforms this situation by treating solar radiation as a common good belonging to the inhabitants of the territories it illuminates — exactly as fisheries, water, and forests have been managed as commons for centuries in the same region.

The framework rests on four integrated layers: a physical model that captures real-time energy scarcity; a pricing mechanism that makes scarcity economically visible; a contract architecture that sells *guaranteed availability* rather than *energy delivered*; and a governance model grounded in one-person-one-vote democratic assemblies, imperative mandates, and a radical transparency principle — **an untraceable act is legally null**.

The Mediterranean geography is not a constraint in this framework. It is its primary asset. The microclimatic decorrelation between island sites — produced by orographic complexity — creates natural portfolio diversification that reduces collective storage requirements and increases system resilience. Corsica, Sardinia, Sicily and Tunisia do not merely add up. They multiply.

The political precedent is the CECA (European Coal and Steel Community, 1951): a common resource governance structure among former adversaries that preceded and made possible the broader European integration. Mare Nostrum proposes a Solar CECA for the twenty-first century Mediterranean — bottom-up, institutionally robust, and anti-messianic by design.

**Five core recommendations** emerge from the analysis, addressed to Mediterranean think tanks, regional governments, and European institutions:

1. Establish territorial Sovereign Solar Funds with one-person-one-vote governance in each signatory territory
2. Adopt mandatory full traceability of all fund acts as a legal condition of validity
3. Constitute a Mediterranean Energy Sovereignty Initiative (MESI) as a contractual inter-territorial coordination layer — not a supranational authority
4. Shift the regulatory unit from kWh delivered to guaranteed availability under scarcity conditions
5. Frame the initiative as a Solar CECA at the June 2026 Mediterranean Energy Forum, with Corte as founding venue

## 1. The Problem: Three Failures in One

### 1.1 The Physical Failure

Mediterranean island grids are structurally fragile. Corsica imports approximately 25% of its electricity from the French mainland via the CIVAR submarine cable — a single point of failure for a population of 350,000. Sardinia and Sicily, though better interconnected with the Italian peninsula, face chronic volatility in renewable integration. Tunisia's extraordinary solar potential — among the highest in the Mediterranean basin — remains largely unexploited for domestic benefit while the country imports fossil fuels at world market prices.

All four territories share the same paradox: they sit beneath one of the world's most reliable solar resources and remain energetically dependent.

### 1.2 The Economic Failure

The economic frameworks governing these territories price energy as a commodity — a flat rate per kilowatt-hour, or at best a time-of-use tariff with limited granularity. This pricing architecture has a fundamental flaw: it treats a kilowatt-hour at 2am in June and a kilowatt-hour at 7am in January as economically equivalent, when their social value differs by orders of magnitude.

The consequence is systematic misinvestment. Production capacity is over-dimensioned for average conditions and under-dimensioned for critical ones. Storage is chronically under-invested because its value — which lies precisely in its availability during rare scarcity events — is not captured by average pricing. The economic signal is wrong, and investment follows it faithfully in the wrong direction.

### 1.3 The Governance Failure

When renewable energy infrastructure is deployed in Mediterranean island territories, the economic rent flows outward: to continental energy corporations, to mainland financial investors, to national grid operators. The inhabitants of Corsica, Sardinia, Sicily and Tunisia — beneath whose sun these installations operate — receive employment during construction, perhaps some local tax revenue, and electricity bills that do not decrease proportionally with the value of what is produced above their heads.

This is not an accident. It is the predictable outcome of governance structures that treat energy as a national or corporate asset rather than a territorial common good. The solution is not regulatory adjustment at the margin. It is a different foundational principle.

## 2. The Framework: Four Integrated Layers

The Mare Nostrum framework addresses the three failures simultaneously through a four-layer architecture in which each layer is a function of a single coupling variable — the instantaneous system scarcity R(t) — ensuring physical, economic, contractual, and governance coherence at all times.

The technical formalization of the framework is maintained as an open document at `github.com/JeanHuguesRobert/marenostrum`. This paper presents the institutional logic; the repository provides the mathematical substrate.

### 2.1 The Physical Layer: Scarcity as a System Variable

Rather than optimizing for average energy production, the Mare Nostrum framework optimizes for *availability under constraint*. The key variable is not total kilowatt-hours produced but the instantaneous gap between what the system can deliver and what inhabitants need — the scarcity pressure R(t).

This single variable drives all subsequent layers. When R(t) is zero, the system is in surplus: energy is abundant, prices are low, storage is charging. When R(t) is positive, the system is under stress: energy is scarce, prices rise, storage discharges, and governance priority rules activate. The framework is designed to perform well precisely in the conditions — high R(t) — where current systems perform worst.

### 2.2 The Economic Layer: Pricing Scarcity, Not Volume

The Mare Nostrum pricing model sets the price of energy as a function of system scarcity rather than production cost. When the system is in surplus, prices approach zero — correctly reflecting the marginal social value of additional solar kilowatt-hours at midday in July. When the system is stressed, prices rise non-linearly — correctly reflecting the disproportionate social cost of energy deprivation during critical periods.

This is not a theoretical abstraction. It is the economic signal that drives the right investment decisions: storage investment is rewarded in proportion to its availability during scarcity, not its throughput during abundance. The framework automatically favors resilience over throughput — the correct optimization criterion for island systems.

### 2.3 The Contract Layer: Selling Availability, Not Energy

The contractual unit in the Mare Nostrum framework is not energy delivered (kWh) but **guaranteed availability under constraint** — a commitment to provide power up to a specified level during specified windows with a specified reliability, even when the system is stressed.

This shift is analogous to the transition from commodity trading to insurance underwriting. The seller commits not to a quantity but to a contingent availability. The price of this commitment is a function of system scarcity statistics — higher for contracts that guarantee delivery during rare, severe scarcity events; lower for contracts that accept interruption during system stress.

Three contract tiers serve different social needs:

- **Market contracts**: no guarantee; interruptible under any stress; lowest cost
- **Standard contracts**: guaranteed availability during moderate stress; intermediate cost
- **Security contracts**: guaranteed availability under extreme conditions; reserved for critical social infrastructure; highest cost

The critical innovation is the priority hierarchy embedded in the contract architecture: when total system capacity is insufficient, Security contracts are honored first, Standard contracts proportionally, and Market contracts last. This hierarchy is not set by the market — it is set by the democratic governance layer and is binding on all operators.

### 2.4 The Governance Layer: Democratic Commons with Radical Transparency

The governance model draws on Elinor Ostrom's foundational work on commons management — the empirical demonstration that communities can sustainably govern shared natural resources through institutional arrangements that embed collective choice, monitoring, and graduated sanctions. Mediterranean communities have practiced exactly this for centuries: fisheries in Marseille, irrigation systems in Valencia, alpine pastures in Corsica itself.

Mare Nostrum institutionalizes this tradition for energy systems through four non-negotiable principles:

**One person, one vote.** The governing assembly of each territorial Sovereign Solar Fund is constituted on the strict basis of one registered beneficiary, one vote. Capital held, income level, duration of residence beyond a minimum threshold — none of these may modify this principle. A governance structure modifiable by capital holders is not a commons; it is a corporation with democratic aesthetics.

**Imperative and revocable mandates.** Administrators are elected on explicit mandates specifying the orientations they are bound to defend. Any documented departure from the mandate constitutes grounds for immediate revocation. Citizens may trigger a revocatory referendum with the support of 5% of registered beneficiaries. The result is binding and immediately enforceable.

**Radical transparency — traceability as law.** This is the most structurally innovative element of the framework. The conventional anti-corruption approach — detect and punish — is reactive, slow, and structurally disadvantaged: corruption always has lead time over investigation.

Mare Nostrum adopts a different architecture:

> **An untraceable act is legally null. Its author is personally liable.**

Every act produced within a Sovereign Solar Fund — decision, contract, payment, delegation, vote — must carry: a certified author identity, an unfalsifiable timestamp, a documented justification, and a reconstructable causal chain to its final execution. An act lacking any of these is void automatically, without judicial decision, and its author bears unlimited personal liability.

This transforms the anti-corruption problem from a detection problem (hard) to a substrate problem (structural). If an opaque act has no legal existence, the gains from corruption are void before any investigation begins.

**Asset inalienability.** Fund infrastructure may not be sold or transferred to entities in which more than 49% of capital is held outside the territory. This clause is perpetual and modifiable only by citizen-initiated referendum — not by administrators, not by external investors, not by national governments.

## 3. The Mediterranean Asset: Decorrelation as Dividend

### 3.1 Why the Mediterranean Geography is the Framework's Key Resource

The standard objection to island renewable systems is intermittency: when the sun doesn't shine on one island, the system fails. This objection is correct for a single-site system. It dissolves for a properly constituted multi-site Mediterranean portfolio.

The Mediterranean basin's orographic complexity — the mountain spines of Corsica, Sardinia, and Sicily; the contrast between Maghreb desert and coastal climates — produces strong microclimatic decorrelation between production profiles across sites separated by modest distances. A tramontane event that depresses production in northern Corsica typically leaves Sardinia's Campidano unaffected. A libeccio saturating Corsica's western coast often clears the eastern Sardinian coast. A winter depression over the Tyrrhenian Sea rarely extends to central Tunisia.

This decorrelation is not a meteorological curiosity. It is a structural portfolio asset, analyzable through the same Markowitz optimization framework used for financial portfolios: the variance of the aggregate production portfolio is substantially lower than the weighted sum of individual site variances. Lower variance means lower required storage for the same reliability guarantee — and lower storage cost means lower cost of Security contracts for inhabitants.

### 3.2 Quantifying the Asset

Preliminary estimates based on PVGIS irradiation data suggest inter-site production correlations in the range of 0.2–0.4 between Corsica and Tunisia — compared to 0.7–0.9 typical of continental grid regions at similar distances. This implies a portfolio variance reduction of 30–50% relative to a single-site reference — a significant reduction in the storage requirement to achieve a given reliability level.

The addition of Tunisia to a Corsica–Sardinia–Sicily portfolio does not merely add production capacity. It adds a qualitatively different production profile — lower winter variance, higher summer floor — that complements the northern island profiles in exactly the season when northern island scarcity is most severe.

### 3.3 The Sovereign Dividend

If the solar resource is treated as a common good and the framework's economic architecture captures its full value — including the scarcity premium from Security contracts — preliminary estimates suggest per-inhabitant annual dividends of the order of:

| Territory | Population | Potential annual dividend |
|-----------|-----------|--------------------------|
| Corsica | 350,000 | €2,500–3,500 / person |
| Sardinia | 1,600,000 | €2,000–2,800 / person |
| Sicily | 4,800,000 | €1,700–2,300 / person |
| Tunisia | 12,000,000 | €2,800–3,800 / person |

These figures assume full deployment of sustainable agrivoltaic capacity, market access to continental European buyers, and Security contract pricing reflecting genuine scarcity value. They are indicative, not projections. They establish the order of magnitude of what is at stake — and what current governance frameworks leave uncaptured.

For Tunisia, the numbers are transformational: a dividend approaching the country's current GDP per capita, funded entirely by a natural resource currently generating negligible domestic benefit.

## 4. The Historical Precedent: A Solar CECA

### 4.1 Why Previous Mediterranean Integration Attempts Failed

The history of Mediterranean integration initiatives is a history of diplomatic ambition without economic substrate:

The **Barcelona Process** (1995) established a Euro-Mediterranean partnership framework but produced no shared economic structures capable of outlasting political friction. The **Union for the Mediterranean** (2008), launched with considerable political investment, was paralyzed within months by the Gaza conflict. The **Mediterranean Solar Plan**, announced in the same year, was never funded. **Desertec**, the ambitious private-sector initiative for North African solar export to Europe, collapsed following the Arab Spring.

The pattern is constant: political architecture first, economic substance never — or much later. When the first geopolitical shock arrives, the diplomatic structure dissolves because it has no substrate of common interest strong enough to resist.

### 4.2 The CECA Model

The exception — the one Mediterranean-adjacent integration that worked — followed the opposite sequence.

The **European Coal and Steel Community** (Paris, 1951) was not a political union. It was a common governance structure for two strategic resources — coal and steel — among six nations that had been at war within living memory. It succeeded because it created a substrate of common economic interest — shared resource governance, shared market, shared investment signals — that made political conflict economically costly for all parties.

Six years later, the same six nations signed the Treaty of Rome.

**Energy common governance preceded and enabled political union.** This sequence — economic commons first, political integration second — is the only sequence for which there is empirical evidence of success in the European context.

Mare Nostrum proposes exactly this sequence for the twenty-first century Mediterranean, with solar energy in the role of coal and steel. The Sovereign Solar Funds of Corsica, Sardinia, Sicily and Tunisia do not need to agree on borders, migration, or foreign policy. They need to agree on one thing: the solar resource above their territories belongs to their inhabitants, and they will govern it together through transparent, democratic, and legally robust institutions.

### 4.3 Why This Time Is Different

Three structural differences make the Mare Nostrum approach more robust than previous attempts:

**The economic interest is local, not corporate.** Previous initiatives (Desertec, Noor, ELMED) routed the solar dividend to corporations and national governments. Mare Nostrum routes it to inhabitants. A Corsican, Sardinian, Sicilian, or Tunisian citizen who receives a monthly dividend from a Sovereign Solar Fund has a direct material interest in the continuation of the framework — an interest that persists through political changes, diplomatic crises, and leadership transitions.

**The governance is anti-fragile.** One-person-one-vote assemblies, imperative mandates, citizen-initiated revocation, and traceability-as-law collectively produce an institution that is resistant to both internal corruption and external capture. This is not idealism — it is institutional engineering, applying lessons from successful commons governance across two millennia of Mediterranean history.

**The technology is now available.** In 1951, distributed democratic governance of a common resource required physical assemblies, paper registries, and slow communication. In 2026, distributed ledger technology, cryptographic timestamping, and real-time open audit systems make the traceability architecture technically implementable at marginal cost. The institutional design is now feasible in a way it was not for previous initiatives.

## 5. Recommendations

### Recommendation 1 — Establish Territorial Sovereign Solar Funds

Each signatory territory (Corsica, Sardinia, Sicily, Tunisia) should establish a Sovereign Solar Fund governed by the principles set out in Section 2.4: one-person-one-vote assembly, imperative and revocable mandates, asset inalienability, and mandatory traceability of all acts.

**Responsible institutions**: regional governments of Corsica, Sardinia, Sicily; Tunisian Ministry of Energy; European Commission (cohesion funds, IPCEI mechanism)

**Enabling instrument**: a multilateral framework agreement establishing minimum governance standards for funds eligible for inter-territorial coordination — not a supranational authority, but a certification mechanism.

### Recommendation 2 — Adopt Traceability as Legal Condition

Mediterranean energy governance frameworks should adopt the principle that **an untraceable energy governance act is legally null**. This requires:

- Mandatory distributed ledger registration of all fund decisions
- Open real-time audit accessible to all beneficiaries
- Personal unlimited liability of fund administrators for untraceable acts
- Reversal of burden of proof: opacity is presumed fraudulent

**Responsible institutions**: national regulators (CRE France, ARERA Italy, STEG Tunisia); European Commission (energy governance directive)

### Recommendation 3 — Constitute MESI as a Contractual Coordination Layer

A **Mediterranean Energy Sovereignty Initiative (MESI)** should be constituted as a contractual inter-territorial coordination layer among Sovereign Solar Funds. MESI is explicitly *not* a supranational authority: it is a pool of bilateral agreements establishing mutual energy availability guarantees, inter-territorial arbitration rules, and a common Security contract standard.

**Responsible institutions**: founding Sovereign Solar Funds; EIB (financing); BEI/AFD (guarantees for Tunisia-side infrastructure)

**Model**: the Nordic energy cooperation framework (Nordpool/Nordisk Råd) adapted to Mediterranean political realities

### Recommendation 4 — Shift the Regulatory Unit to Guaranteed Availability

Mediterranean energy regulators should progressively replace kWh-based tariff frameworks with **guaranteed availability contracts** parameterized by reliability coefficient and scarcity exposure. This requires:

- Definition of a standard Mediterranean availability contract format
- Regulatory recognition of Security contracts for critical social infrastructure
- Pricing frameworks that reflect scarcity-weighted value rather than average production cost

**Responsible institutions**: ENTSO-E; national regulators; European Commission (Clean Energy Package revision)

### Recommendation 5 — Launch the Mediterranean Solar Charter

A **Charter of the Mediterranean Sun** — establishing the foundational principles of solar common good governance — should be publicly proclaimed at a Mediterranean venue in summer 2026, co-signed symbolically by representatives of each signatory territory, and submitted as a joint position paper to the European Commission's Mediterranean Dialogue framework.

The Charter's foundational text is available in open draft at `github.com/JeanHuguesRobert/mareostrum`, in Corsican, Sardinian, Sicilian, Tunisian Arabic, and bridge versions in French, Italian, and English. Its multilingual architecture is itself a political statement: no language is dominant, because no territory is.

**Responsible institutions**: Institut Mariani R&D; regional governments; civil society organizations in each signatory territory

## 6. Conclusion

The Mediterranean is not an energy problem. It is an energy asset — one of the world's most consistent solar resources, diversified by microclimatic complexity across islands and shores whose production profiles naturally complement each other. The problem is not physical. It is institutional: the architecture that currently governs this asset was designed to extract value from the territory, not to return it to the inhabitants.

Mare Nostrum proposes a different architecture — one grounded in democratic commons governance, radical transparency, and the recognition that solar radiation is a natural gift whose economic value belongs to the people it illuminates.

The CECA transformed former adversaries into economic partners in six years. It did so not through diplomatic genius but through structural alignment of economic interest around a common resource. The solar endowment of the Mediterranean is a far larger resource than the coalfields of the Ruhr — and the structural alignment it can produce is correspondingly more powerful.

The question is not whether this is possible. It is whether the institutions exist to make it happen. This paper is a contribution to building them.
